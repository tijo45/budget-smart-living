What Is Student Loan Refinancing? (and How It Works)

Student loan refinancing is straightforward: a private lender pays off your existing student loans and issues you a brand-new loan with (ideally) a lower interest rate, different repayment term, or both. You go from making payments to your current lender(s) to making a single payment to the new lender.

Example: If you owe $50,000 at 7% interest across three different loans, a refinance might consolidate those into one $50,000 loan at 4.5% ‚Äî saving you $73/month and $8,700+ in total interest over 10 years.

Federal vs. Private Loans ‚Äî Which Can You Refinance?

You can refinance both federal and private student loans through private refinancing. You can also combine federal and private loans into a single refinanced loan. However, refinancing federal loans with a private lender means permanently losing federal benefits ‚Äî so think carefully before refinancing federal loans (more on this below).

Should You Refinance Your Student Loans?

When Refinancing Makes Sense

Refinancing is likely a smart move if:

  • Your current rate is above 5% and you have good credit (670+) ‚Äî you'll likely qualify for a significantly lower rate
  • You have private student loans ‚Äî there are no federal protections to lose
  • Your income is stable and secure ‚Äî you don't need income-driven repayment as a safety net
  • You don't work in public service ‚Äî so PSLF isn't applicable
  • Your credit score has improved since graduation ‚Äî you may now qualify for rates 2-4% lower than your original loans
  • You have multiple loans and want one payment ‚Äî simplification plus potential rate savings

When You Should NOT Refinance

‚ö†Ô∏è Warning: Do NOT refinance federal student loans if any of these apply:
  • You're pursuing Public Service Loan Forgiveness (PSLF) ‚Äî refinancing disqualifies you permanently
  • You're on an income-driven repayment plan and rely on the lower payments
  • Your income is unstable ‚Äî federal loans offer forbearance and deferment; private loans are less flexible
  • You expect future federal relief programs ‚Äî only federal loans would qualify
  • Your credit score is below 650 ‚Äî you likely won't get a better rate through refinancing

How to Refinance Student Loans ‚Äî Step-by-Step

Step 1: Check Your Credit Score & DTI Ratio

Before applying, know where you stand. You can check your credit score for free through Credit Karma, your bank's app, or annualcreditreport.com. Most lenders want:

  • Credit score: 650 minimum, 700+ for the best rates
  • Debt-to-income ratio (DTI): Below 50% (total monthly debt payments √∑ gross monthly income)
  • Steady income: Employment history or consistent self-employment income

If your credit score needs work, spend 3‚Äì6 months improving it before applying. Even a 30‚Äì50 point increase can save you thousands.

Step 2: Gather Your Loan Details

You'll need the following for each loan you want to refinance:

  • Current lender name and account number
  • Remaining balance
  • Current interest rate
  • Monthly payment amount
  • Whether the loan is federal or private

For federal loans, find all this on StudentAid.gov. For private loans, check your lender's website or your most recent statement.

Step 3: Get Rate Quotes from 3‚Äì5 Lenders (Soft Pull)

This is the most important step. Check your rate with at least 3 lenders to ensure you're getting the best deal. All lenders on our list use a soft credit pull for rate checks ‚Äî it takes 2 minutes and has absolutely zero impact on your credit score.

Our recommended approach:

  1. Start with SoFi (typically has the lowest rates)
  2. Check Credible (compares 8+ lenders at once)
  3. Check Earnest (especially if you want flexible terms)

Step 4: Compare Offers (APR, Term, Monthly Payment)

When comparing offers, focus on these numbers:

  • APR (Annual Percentage Rate): The true cost of the loan including fees. Lower is better.
  • Monthly payment: Make sure it fits your budget comfortably
  • Total cost of the loan: Monthly payment √ó number of months. A lower monthly payment with a longer term can cost MORE total.
  • Loan term: Shorter terms = higher monthly payments but less total interest paid
üí∞ Pro Tip: Choose the shortest loan term you can comfortably afford. A 10-year term at 4.5% saves you significantly more than a 20-year term at the same rate ‚Äî even though the monthly payment is higher.

Step 5: Choose Your Lender & Apply

Once you've compared offers, select the lender with the best overall package (lowest APR, best features, strong customer service). The formal application takes 15‚Äì30 minutes and typically requires:

  • Government-issued ID
  • Proof of income (pay stubs, W-2, or tax returns)
  • Proof of employment
  • Loan statements from current lenders
  • Degree verification (some lenders)

Step 6: Complete Verification & Sign

After submitting your application, the lender will verify your information (3‚Äì7 business days). Once approved, review the final loan terms carefully before signing. After signing, the new lender pays off your old loans directly (3‚Äì10 business days). Continue making payments on your old loans until you receive confirmation that they've been paid off.

FAQs About Student Loan Refinancing

Does refinancing student loans hurt your credit?

Checking your rates uses a soft pull ‚Äî zero credit impact. You can get quotes from multiple lenders safely. The formal application involves a hard pull, which may temporarily lower your score by 5‚Äì10 points. This recovers within a few months, and refinancing can actually improve your credit long-term by lowering your DTI ratio.

Can you refinance student loans more than once?

Yes, there's no limit. If rates drop significantly (0.50%+) or your credit improves, refinancing again can save additional money. Many borrowers refinance 2‚Äì3 times over the life of their loans.

Should I refinance federal student loans?

Only if you won't need income-driven repayment, PSLF, or federal forbearance protections. If you have stable income, good credit, and don't work in public service, refinancing federal loans at a lower rate can save thousands. If you're uncertain, keep federal loans federal.

What credit score do I need to refinance student loans?

Most lenders require 650+, but 700+ gets you the best rates. A cosigner with strong credit can help you qualify or get a lower rate if your score is borderline.

How much money can you save by refinancing?

Reducing your rate by 2% on a $50,000 loan saves approximately $7,500 over 10 years. On $100,000, that same reduction saves $15,000+. Even a 1% reduction on $50K saves roughly $2,700.

Can I refinance student loans with a cosigner?

Yes. A cosigner with good credit (700+) can help you qualify or get a lower rate. Most lenders offer cosigner release after 12‚Äì48 months of on-time payments. SoFi and ELFI both offer cosigner release options.

How long does student loan refinancing take?

Typically 2‚Äì4 weeks: rate check (2 minutes), full application (15‚Äì30 minutes), lender review (3‚Äì7 business days), and disbursement (3‚Äì10 business days). Keep paying your current loans until the process is complete.
