Important Cashback Card Rules

Cashback cards are only valuable if you use them responsibly. Here are the non-negotiable rules:

  1. Pay your balance in full every month. The average credit card APR is 20-28%. Carrying a $1,000 balance for a year costs $200-280 in interest ‚Äî wiping out all your cashback earnings. If you can't pay in full, focus on paying down existing debt first.
  2. Don't spend more just to earn rewards. Cashback is a bonus on purchases you'd make anyway. A 2% reward on a $500 purchase you didn't need costs you $490.
  3. Set up autopay for the full statement balance. This prevents missed payments (which trash your credit score) and interest charges.
  4. Track your spending with a budgeting app. Cards make spending feel less "real." Use an app to stay within your budget.

Frequently Asked Questions

What credit score do you need for a cashback credit card?

Most top cashback cards require a "Good" credit score of 670 or higher. The Discover it Cash Back is more lenient and may approve applicants with scores in the 640-670 range. If your score is below 640, consider a secured credit card to build credit first, then upgrade to a cashback card in 6-12 months.

Is 2% cash back good?

Yes ‚Äî 2% flat-rate cash back with no annual fee is excellent for a general-purpose card. On average household spending of $30,000-60,000/year, that's $600-1,200 in free money annually. Category-specific cards can earn more (3-5%) in certain areas, but 2% on everything is hard to beat for simplicity.

Can I have multiple cashback credit cards?

Absolutely. Many savvy cardholders use a 2-3 card strategy: one card for bonus categories (dining, groceries) and one flat-rate card for everything else. This maximizes your total cashback without much extra effort. Just make sure you can manage payments on all cards responsibly.

Do cashback credit cards charge annual fees?

All seven cards in our top picks charge $0 annual fees. Premium cashback cards with annual fees (like the Amex Blue Cash Preferred at $95/year) exist but only make sense if you spend enough in bonus categories to offset the fee. For most people, no-annual-fee cards offer the best value.
